{% markdown %} ## What this means Designers consider accessibility and inclusive design to ensure that everyone can access and use products, services, and environments, regardless of their abilities or disabilities. They do this by thinking about how people with different needs, such as visual or hearing impairments, physical disabilities, or learning difficulties, might interact with their designs.
Find out how many of your users might have a disability
To achieve this, designers take a user-centered approach, which involves involving people with disabilities in the design process and testing their designs with a diverse range of users. They also follow guidelines and standards, such as the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) or the Disability Discrimination Act (DDA), to ensure that their designs meet the needs of all users. Designers also consider factors such as color contrast, font size, and readability to ensure that their designs are easy to read and understand. They may also use assistive technologies, such as screen readers, to test their designs and ensure that they are compatible with these tools.
Conducting user research

Designers may involve people with disabilities in their research to gain insights into their needs, preferences, and challenges.

This helps designers to understand the user's perspective and design products, services, and environments that are accessible and inclusive.

- Work with user researchers to understand user needs and get involved in research activity - run design crits, peer reviews, and 2i content and designs with people outside of your team

Considering the user experience: Designers consider the user's experience when interacting with their designs. They think about how people with different abilities, such as visual or hearing impairments, physical disabilities, or cognitive limitations, might interact with their designs. This helps them to identify barriers and make adjustments to ensure that everyone can access and use their designs. - learn about your users and the environment and situations they use your services, don't design for one scenario - Limit your own cognitive biases by co-designing or collaborating and de-risking your design with users and other designers 4. Using assistive technologies: Designers may use assistive technologies, such as screen readers or alternative input devices, to test their designs and ensure that they are compatible with these tools.
